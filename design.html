Design

I became interested in web design and development as a means to an end, namely understanding and improving our store's website: grothmusic.com. Like the ecommerce sites of many businesses, grothmusic.com utilizes a third party shopping cart platform. The platform is proprietary, but in turn relies a variety of other frameworks (ASP.NET, Bootstrap), libraries (Jquery), and APIs (Google reCAPTCHA, Analytics, Web Fonts, etc.). It serves its purpose, but its one-size-fits-all design has limitations. As I tried to customize it to the unique needs of our classical sheet music business, I found it to be rigid, complex, secret, and, perhaps worst of all, slow.

Over the course of the pandemic year, and with vital help from a professional developer named Martin Cambronne for back-end issues, I have gradually made measurable progress improving the speed, functionality, and design of our site. For me, a musician with no coding experience, this entailed learning HTML, CSS, a smattering of Javascript and JQuery, and the concepts behind ASP.NET MVC and SQL databases. It required learning some tools of the trade, namely VS Code, source control (Bitbucket), Inkscape, WebPageTest, PageSpeed Insights, Google Search Console and Analytics, and so on.

Actually, improving the performance of grothmusic.com turned out to largely be an exercise in deconstruction, figuring out which aspects of the shopping cart platform were extraneous or redundant (or even nefarious) and then removing or reducing them without breaking anything. We also added code for things like structured data, a better (and free) search, surfacing previously hidden data on category pages (composer!), and many other small but cumulatively important changes. Again, the heavy lifting on anything that involved the database was done by Martin.

A few weeks ago this blog post by Steren Giannini appeared in my feed: My stack will outlive yours. After struggling against the layers of complexity involved with the Groth site, Steren's approach resonated with me: HTML+CSS, simple as that. I decided to apply it to my vanity website. I am also using Github Pages as my host (as does he), which has allowed me to abandon my former webhost and save $96/year.
